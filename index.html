Behind the numbers

What the board is reading

The same approach, checked against two centuries of racing and scored only on years it had never seen, sits behind every figure above. Three things stand out.

What separates two crews

How you've rowed this week counts most

Each factor's pull on the odds of catching the boat ahead, per standard deviation, with the rest held level. How a crew has rowed so far this week outweighs anything you knew before it started — last year's finish, a Blue or two aboard, how much of the squad it kept.

How far ahead you can see

Wednesday is a coin toss; Saturday isn't

Crews are seeded by last year's order, so on the first day the boat ahead is the one most like you — and the result is close to a flip. Each day's racing sorts the genuinely quick from the rest: the model goes from barely better than a guess to picking the bumping crew in better than five times in six, and its read on each crew's speed tightens with every result.

Skill and the draw

You can't fluke blades

With every crew equally fast, four bumps in a row would be about 9× rarer than it actually is — so blades take genuine speed. But the rest is the draw. Whether you bump turns mostly on the crew ahead: caught and sitting in front of you, you go through about three times in five; bumped out and clear up the river, nearer one in twenty. Which is why blades barely repeat — 84% of this year's winners didn't have them last year.

How this works, and what it can't tell you

Where the numbers come from

Every Oxford bumps race, from two records kept independently — Anu Dudhia's archive and bumps.live — which agree on 99.99% of the 20,617 races they both cover, back to 1826. Crew lists, coxes and coaches (2007 on) come from bumps.live, Oxford Blues from Wikipedia's Boat Race tables, and the weather from the Radcliffe Observatory.

How the predictions are made

Each crew is given an estimate of its speed — its skill — as a Bayesian belief with a margin of error. The estimate starts from the seeding, last year's finish, Torpids form and any Blues aboard, and is updated by each day's results, so it sharpens as the week goes on; how much of the squad a crew kept from last year sets how tightly it's pinned, not where it starts. To turn skills into the chances on the board, the gap between two crews is fed to a simple race simulator: a bigger gap means a faster catch, which is how over-bumps and the occasional run of blades fall out. The simulator honours the real rules — a crew can only catch the boat it is actually chasing, and divisions are joined only by the sandwich boat — so a fast crew several places back can't leapfrog the boats between it and its target. Tap a crew to see what's tilting its number.

What it can't tell you

The model can't see who's injured, ill or flying this week, so take any single number as a lean, not a verdict. "Blues" means the openweight Blue Boat only — reserves and lightweights aren't in the data yet. And races before 1900, where the old records get patchy, are left out of the headline figures.
