Behind the numbers

What the board is reading

One model, trained on two centuries of racing and scored only on years it had never seen, produces every figure above. Three things about it are worth a look.

What separates two crews

How you've rowed this week counts most

Each factor's pull on the odds of catching the boat ahead, per standard deviation, with the rest held level. How a crew has gone over the first days outweighs everything pre-race — last year's finish, a Blue or two aboard, even how many of last year's rowers it kept.

How far ahead you can see

Wednesday is a coin toss; Saturday isn't

Crews are seeded by last year's order, so on the first day the boat ahead is the one most like you — and the result is close to a flip. Each day of racing sorts the genuinely quick from the rest, and by Saturday the model is sharp.

Skill and the draw

Blades are earned, not fluked

Simulate a season where every crew is identical and blades almost never happen. They occur about 11× more often — you can't fluke four bumps. But the draw gates it, and it barely depends on you: if the boat you're chasing is caught and sits in front of you, you go through about 3 in 5 times; if it bumps out and pulls up the river, your chance falls to about 1 in 20. So 86% of crews that blade hadn't the year before — speed buys the ticket, the draw completes the set.

How this was done

Data

Two independent records of every Oxford bumps race — Anu Dudhia's archive and the data behind bumps.live — reconstructed separately and kept only where they agree, which they do on 99.99% of the 20,617 crew-races they share (1826–2026). Crews, coxes and coaches (2007 on) come from bumps.live; Oxford Blues from Wikipedia's Boat Race tables; weather from the Radcliffe Meteorological Station.

The model

The board is a single head-to-head model: for each adjacent pair it estimates the chance the chasing crew catches the boat ahead, from the difference between the two crews (form this week, last year's finish, same-year Torpids, Blues aboard, squad continuity) and the target's own pace. It is validated by walk-forward — trained only on the past, scored on later years it never saw — at an AUC of about 0.81, a Brier skill of 0.23 over a "nothing changes" baseline, and was independently reproduced by a separate analysis. Almost all of that predictive power is current-week form; the pre-race factors separate crews but add little once the week's racing is known. Each prediction is decomposed into the factors behind it.

Skill versus luck

A mechanistic simulator of the chase — including the rule that a crew whose target bumps out loses its bump — is calibrated to reproduce the real rates of bumps, blades and spoons, then re-run to see how outcomes scatter when only the draw changes. Stability across years is reported in places moved and transition rates, never as a correlation (which is ~0.99 by construction across seven divisions and says nothing).

Caveats

"Blues" counts the openweight Blue Boat only; reserves and lightweights aren't yet in the data. Factor effects are associations with confounders removed as far as the data allows — strong evidence, not proof. Results before 1900 are excluded from the headline figures.
