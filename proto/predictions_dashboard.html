Each boat is shown chasing the crew directly ahead — the natural unit of a bumps race. The bar is the modelled chance it closes the gap and bumps on . The Head of the River has no boat to chase; the bottom boat cannot be caught. The model knows position, form and squad — not who is injured or flying this week, so read a single number as a tendency.

Why the board says what it says

What the model is actually reading

One head-to-head model produces every number above, trained on two centuries of races and scored on years it had never seen. Three things about it are worth knowing.

What separates two crews

Form this week dwarfs everything

Each factor's pull on the odds of bumping the boat ahead, per standard deviation, holding the rest fixed. How a crew has rowed this week moves the needle more than three times over — far more than last year's form or a Blue in the boat.

It isn't momentum

Crews reveal their speed, they don't ride a streak

Day one is a coin toss; by Friday the model is sharp. The lift is information, not a hot hand: "bumped yesterday" looks worth ×6.8 on the odds, but control for how good the crew is on its other days and it collapses to ×1.7. Roughly three-quarters of apparent momentum is just speed becoming visible.

Skill vs the draw

Blades can't be fluked — but barely repeat

Simulate a world where every crew is equal and blades almost never happen. In reality they occur about 11× as often — you cannot stumble into four bumps. Yet 86% of crews that win blades hadn't the year before: speed buys a ticket, the draw decides which fast crew completes the set.
