Bumps results are noisy: a crew can climb on a clean bump, stall behind a slow boat, or get caught through no fault of its own. So which colleges are *genuinely* fast? One model reads every race on record — Torpids and Eights, every day since 2007 — as evidence about a single thing: how quick each crew really is. It's a strength rating, not a forecast, and a different lens on the same sport as the live board.

How a crew's strength is estimated

Every race is one clue. Each day, a boat either catches the crew ahead or it doesn't — and the closer the two are in strength, the closer the result. The model takes all ~20,000 of those contests at once and asks: what single set of crew strengths makes everything that actually happened most likely?

Strength travels with the rowers. A crew isn't judged in isolation each year. Its strength is tied to the boats its rowers came from — last year's crew, this spring's Torpids crew, the four who moved up from the second VIII — so last year's form, Torpids and squad continuity all feed in through one rule: shared rowers carry strength. (The model even learns the shape of that carry: a core of returning rowers turns out to anchor a crew slightly more than its headcount alone would suggest.)

"On a given day" is just the best read using every race up to then. Before racing starts, a crew's strength is its history. Each day's result is fresh evidence and the estimate is nudged — bump a strong crew and it rises, get caught and it falls — and its margin of error shrinks with every race. A crew with a long, settled track record is pinned tightly; a brand-new crew, or one early in the week, carries a wide band, which is why two crews with overlapping bands are a real toss-up.

Method, and what it can't tell you

One Bayesian generative model treats every adjacent contest in every race as an observation of a latent skill field; strength is carried between a college's boats and seasons by the rowers they share (from crew lists, 2007 on), with the carry-over shape learned from the data. Fit by variational inference and checked against MCMC — the skill estimates agree to r≈0.99. Skills are comparable only within a gender; the absolute level is arbitrary, so everything here is shown relative to the field that year.

An honesty note: on a strict, leak-free, like-for-like test, this richer model is statistically indistinguishable from the simpler model behind the live board at actually predicting the next day's bumps. It is offered here as an interpretive strength rating — a way to ask "who's really fast, adjusting for the draw" — not as a better forecaster. Current-season ratings in particular are small-sample and should be read with their uncertainty.
